How the Impeachment Process Works

As President Trump faces the threat of impeachment again, questions arise as to how the process works.


What is Impeachment?

Impeachment was set forth in the Constitution to give Congress the power to remove a president from office before their term has ended. The Constitution cites specific actions for impeachment and removal from office. However, only two presidents, Andrew Johnson (1868) and Bill Clinton (1998), have ever been impeached, and neither were removed from office.


What Causes Impeachment?

According the Constitution, "treason, bribery, or other high crimes and misdemeanors" are impeachable offenses. High crimes and misdemeanors essentially means the abuse of power by a high-level public official. It does not necessarily have to be a violation of an ordinary criminal statute.


Who is Involved?

The impeachment process resides in Congress and reaches across both houses.


What are the Steps of the Impeachment Process?

Step
One

An Impeachment Resolution

First, the Speaker of the House must direct special committees to investigate the proposed impeachable offenses. These committees will present evidence to the US House Committee on the Judiciary (Judiciary Committee).

Step Two

Resolution Approval from Judiciary Committee

After reviewing cases from the special committees, a simple majority vote from the Judiciary committee will approve the resolution to be sent to the House for a vote.

Step Three

House of Representatives Vote

Once the vote reaches the House, a vote is held on one or more articles of impeachment. If a simple majority is reached, then the president is impeached.

Step Four

Trial in the Senate

Once the president has been impeached, a trial is held in the Senate to determine if the president committed a crime. The trial is presided over by the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, and members of the House serve as managers to present evidence during the trial. To convict the president of the crime, the Senate would need a two-thirds majority.
